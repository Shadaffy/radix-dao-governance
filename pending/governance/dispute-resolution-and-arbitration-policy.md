# Dispute Resolution & Arbitration Policy

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines how disputes within the DAO are:

* identified
* escalated
* resolved

It ensures disputes are handled:

* fairly
* transparently
* without centralizing authority

---

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to disputes involving:

* Working Groups
* Authorized Signers
* RAC members
* governance processes
* proposal interpretation
* execution disagreements

---

## 3. Guiding Principles

### 3.1 DAO Sovereignty

Final authority always rests with the DAO.

---

### 3.2 Minimal Escalation

Disputes should be resolved at the lowest level possible.

---

### 3.3 Transparency

Disputes and outcomes must be documented.

---

### 3.4 Neutral Facilitation

No actor should judge disputes they are involved in.

---

### 3.5 Proportionality

Resolution mechanisms should match the severity of the dispute.

---

## 4. Types of Disputes

---

### 4.1 Operational Disputes

Examples:

* Working Group disagreements
* coordination issues
* delivery conflicts

---

### 4.2 Execution Disputes

Examples:

* signer refusal to execute
* disagreement on proposal interpretation
* delays in execution

---

### 4.3 Governance Disputes

Examples:

* proposal validity challenges
* voting process concerns
* veto disagreements

---

### 4.4 Misconduct Disputes

Examples:

* abuse of role
* conflict of interest violations
* negligence

---

## 5. Resolution Levels

Disputes are resolved through escalating levels:

---

### Level 1: Direct Resolution

Parties attempt to resolve directly.

Requirements:

* good faith discussion
* documented communication

---

### Level 2: Working Group Resolution

If unresolved:

* relevant Working Group(s) attempt mediation
* Strategic Coordination WG may assist

---

### Level 3: RAC Review

If still unresolved:

* RAC reviews the dispute
* ensures process compliance
* provides a formal interpretation or recommendation

RAC does NOT impose binding outcomes beyond procedural enforcement.

---

### Level 4: DAO Decision

If escalation continues:

* dispute is submitted as a governance proposal
* token holders decide

This is the final authority.

---

## 6. Emergency Disputes

If a dispute involves:

* security risk
* treasury risk
* urgent operational threat

Then:

* Emergency & Safeguards Policy applies
* RAC may take temporary action

---

## 7. Signer Dispute Handling

If a signer refuses execution:

### Valid grounds:

* proposal inconsistency
* missing authorization
* legal or security risk

---

### Process:

1. Signer documents refusal
2. Issue reviewed by Working Group
3. Escalated to RAC if unresolved
4. DAO vote if necessary

---

## 8. Governance Interpretation

If ambiguity exists in governance documents:

* RAC provides interpretation
* interpretation may be challenged via DAO vote

---

## 9. Misconduct Handling

If misconduct is alleged:

* evidence must be presented
* RAC may review
* DAO may vote on removal or sanctions

---

## 10. Arbitration Limits

No entity may:

* unilaterally impose binding outcomes
* override DAO authority
* act as final judge outside governance

---

## 11. Documentation

All disputes must include:

* summary of issue
* parties involved
* actions taken
* outcome

Sensitive details may be partially confidential if required.

---

## 12. Abuse Prevention

To prevent misuse of dispute processes:

* frivolous disputes may be dismissed
* repeated abuse may lead to governance action
* thresholds for escalation may apply

---

## 13. Interaction with Veto Mechanism

* veto is a specific governance safeguard
* disputes do not automatically trigger veto
* veto rules remain defined in Proposal Framework

---

## 14. Timeframes

Defined in DAO Parameters:

* response times
* escalation windows
* resolution expectations

---

## 15. Amendments

This policy may be updated via governance.

---

## 16. Guiding Principle

> Disputes should be resolved with structure,
> not power.
